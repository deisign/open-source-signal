# Editorial policy

**Open Source Signal / Сигнал відкритих джерел** is a bilingual OSINT journal with a Ukrainian accountability lens and an international horizon.

The project is not a catalogue of tools and not a surveillance manual. It is an editorial filter for public-interest OSINT: verification, investigations, war-crimes documentation, geolocation, platform research, digital safety, infrastructure signals and accountability.

## Core editorial focus

Priority should be given to materials that help readers understand or practice:

1. Ukrainian accountability OSINT.
2. Identification and verification of Russian war-crime suspects.
3. Public-interest investigations of Russian military units, commanders, propagandists, occupation officials and collaborators.
4. OSINT methods for documenting war crimes and preserving evidence.
5. Counting, verifying and mapping Russian KIA, POW, MIA, wounded and missing soldiers.
6. Regional patterns of Russian losses, mobilization, captivity and disappearances.
7. Ukrainian OSINT groups, investigative media and accountability organizations.
8. Telegram as a primary source environment for war-related OSINT.

International OSINT remains in scope when it offers methods, tools, investigative cases, safety lessons or comparative accountability value.

## Language handling

The main stream will often be English-language OSINT material adapted into Ukrainian.

When the source is English or another non-Ukrainian language, the Ukrainian version must be an editorial adaptation, not a literal translation. It should be clear enough for Ukrainian readers who may not read the original.

When the source is Ukrainian-only, do the reverse operation: create a clear English editorial adaptation or synthesis for international readers. Preserve Ukrainian context, but explain institutions, project names, abbreviations, geography, war-specific context and accountability significance.

Do not produce machine-style parallel translations. Both language versions must read like edited publication text.

If a term has no good translation, keep the original term and explain it briefly.

## Evidence and attribution language

For alleged perpetrators, distinguish:

- alleged;
- identified by investigators;
- sanctioned;
- charged;
- convicted.

For casualty, captivity and missing-persons data, distinguish:

- confirmed by open sources;
- listed by an official Ukrainian project;
- reported by relatives;
- statistically estimated;
- unverified.

Do not turn an OSINT identification into a judicial verdict. Do not turn a source pointer into proof.

## Safety boundaries

Do not publish:

- doxxing;
- stalking;
- illegal access;
- deanonymization of private persons;
- credential hunting;
- stolen-data workflows;
- live targeting;
- home addresses;
- private phone numbers;
- family data;
- personal documents;
- revenge-oriented calls;
- operational targeting guidance;
- questionable OSINT bots or “find anyone by phone/face/address” services.

If a source contains personal data or accusation-heavy material, treat it as a pointer requiring independent verification, not as standalone proof.

## Telegram material

Telegram-derived material is candidate material, not automatically reliable. Prefer stable public archives, primary footage/documents, channel posts that can be linked, and claims that can be cross-checked through official records, geolocation, imagery or multiple independent sources.

Telegram leads should be marked internally when they need verification before publication.
